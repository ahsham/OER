Abstract

We investigate the relationship between employee workplace satisfaction and customer service experience in the airline industry by analyzing 42,599 reviews from two independent sources: Glassdoor (9,351 employee reviews) and Skytrax (approximately 33,000 customer reviews). Using Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) topic modeling, we identified key themes in both employee workplace experiences and customer service perceptions. We then examined whether airlines with higher employee satisfaction also achieve higher customer satisfaction.

Our analysis of 10 major international airlines reveals a moderate positive correlation between employee and customer satisfaction (Pearson r=0.56, p=0.095). Airlines with above-median employee ratings achieved customer satisfaction scores approximately 0.4 points higher (on a 10-point scale) than those with below-median employee ratings (t=2.30, p=0.071). Topic modeling revealed that employee complaints about management quality, career opportunities, and workload predict lower employee ratings (R¬≤=0.063), while customer complaints about delays, refunds, and service quality predict lower customer ratings (R¬≤=0.054).

These findings provide empirical support for the service-profit chain theory, suggesting that airlines investing in employee satisfaction may realize benefits in customer satisfaction. However, the small sample size necessitates cautious interpretation and calls for replication with larger datasets.

1. Introduction and Research Question

1.1 Motivation

The airline industry presents a unique context for studying the employee-customer satisfaction relationship. Unlike many service industries, airline employees work in highly constrained environments (aircraft cabins, gate areas) under significant time pressure, often dealing with stressed customers. The quality of service delivery depends heavily on frontline employees‚Äîcabin crew, gate agents, ground staff‚Äîwho directly interface with passengers.

Service management theory, particularly the service-profit chain framework (Heskett et al., 1994), posits that employee satisfaction drives service quality, which in turn drives customer satisfaction and ultimately profitability. Yet empirical evidence for this chain in the airline context remains limited, largely because obtaining both employee and customer data for the same organizations is challenging. Airlines rarely publicize internal employee satisfaction metrics, and even when they conduct surveys, results are proprietary.

The emergence of public review platforms‚ÄîGlassdoor for employee reviews and Skytrax for customer reviews‚Äîcreates a new opportunity to test this theoretical relationship using large-scale, authentic data from real employees and customers rather than laboratory surveys.

1.2 Research Question

Do airlines with higher employee workplace satisfaction achieve higher customer service satisfaction?

To answer this question, we needed to:

  1. Collect employee workplace reviews for multiple airlines
  2. Collect customer flight experience reviews for the same airlines
  3. Calculate aggregate satisfaction metrics for each airline from both perspectives
  4. Test whether these metrics correlate
  5. Identify what specific factors drive employee and customer satisfaction

1.3 Contribution

This research makes three contributions:

  • Methodological: We demonstrate how publicly available review data can be combined to test organizational hypotheses that previously required access to proprietary internal data.
  • Empirical: We provide evidence for the employee-customer satisfaction link using real-world data from 42,599 reviews across 497 airlines, substantially larger than typical survey-based studies.
  • Practical: We identify specific themes (management quality, career development, workload) that predict employee dissatisfaction and may ultimately affect customer experience, offering actionable insights for airline management.

2.2 Why We Used These Specific Methods

Method 1: Topic Modeling (LDA) ‚Äî Understanding What People Say

Purpose

We needed to understand what employees and customers actually talk about in their reviews. With thousands of text reviews, manual reading isn't feasible. Topic modeling automatically discovers recurring themes in large text corpora.

Why LDA Specifically?

  • Unsupervised: LDA doesn't require pre-labeled training data, making it ideal for exploratory research
  • Interpretable: LDA produces topics as probability distributions over words, which humans can interpret (e.g., a topic with high probability for "salary," "pay," "compensation" clearly relates to compensation issues)
  • Probabilistic: Unlike simple word clustering, LDA models each document as a mixture of topics, reflecting the reality that a single review often touches on multiple themes
  • Standard: LDA is widely used in organizational research, making our approach comparable to prior studies

What LDA Does

LDA assumes each review is generated by:

  1. First choosing a mixture of topics (e.g., 60% "management issues," 30% "salary concerns," 10% "travel benefits")
  2. Then for each word position, choosing a topic from that mixture
  3. Then choosing a word from that topic's word distribution

The algorithm works backwards: given the observed words, infer the most likely topic structure that would have generated them.

Mathematical formulation: For each document d: Œ∏d ~ Dirichlet(Œ±) # topic distribution for document For each word position n: zd,n ~ Multinomial(Œ∏d) # choose topic wd,n ~ Multinomial(œÜzd,n) # choose word from that topic Where: Œ∏d = topic mixture for document d (K-dimensional) Œ± = prior controlling topic sparsity (we used Œ± = 50/K) zd,n = topic assignment for word n wd,n = observed word œÜk = word distribution for topic k (V-dimensional) Œ≤ = prior controlling word sparsity (we used Œ≤ = 0.01) K = number of topics (we tested 5-8 topics) V = vocabulary size (~5,000-10,000 unique words)

Purpose

After identifying topics, we needed to know: Do these topics actually matter? Does mentioning "poor management" predict lower employee ratings? Does mentioning "lost baggage" predict lower customer ratings?

Why Regression?

  • Controls for other topics: By including all topics simultaneously, we isolate each topic's unique effect

  • Model fit metrics: R¬≤ tells us what percentage of rating variance the topics explain

Algorithm 3: Missing Data Handling

Input: Review with potentially missing fields Output: Decision (keep/exclude) and imputation where appropriate Employee Reviews: For each employee review r: Required fields: - overall_rating (1-5) - At least one of: pros_text OR cons_text If r.rating is missing: ‚Üí Exclude review (cannot analyze without dependent variable) If r.pros is missing AND r.cons is missing: ‚Üí Exclude review (no text to analyze) If r.pros is missing but r.cons exists: ‚Üí Keep review, set pros = empty string If r.cons is missing but r.pros exists: ‚Üí Keep review, set cons = empty string Optional fields (job_title, location, date): ‚Üí Keep as-is, code missing as NA Customer Reviews: For each customer review r: Required fields: - overall_rating (1-10) If r.overall_rating is missing: ‚Üí Exclude review Detailed ratings (seat_comfort, food, entertainment, etc.): If missing: ‚Üí Code as NA (not null) ‚Üí Do not impute (absence is informative) Review text: If missing: ‚Üí Keep review if ratings exist ‚Üí Exclude from text analysis only Metadata (route, aircraft, date_flown): ‚Üí Keep as-is, code missing as NA Exclusion summary: - Employee reviews excluded: <2% (missing ratings or all text) - Customer reviews excluded: <3% (missing overall rating)

Input: Raw review text (string) Output: Cleaned token list suitable for LDA Procedure: For each review text T: STEP 1: Tokenization tokens = split(T, delimiters=[space, punctuation]) Example: "Great flight! Very comfortable." ‚Üí ["Great", "flight", "!", "Very", "comfortable", "."] STEP 2: Lowercase conversion tokens = [t.lower() for t in tokens] Example: ["Great", "flight"] ‚Üí ["great", "flight"] STEP 3: Punctuation removal tokens = [t for t in tokens if t.isalpha()] Example: ["great", "flight", "!"] ‚Üí ["great", "flight"] STEP 4: Number removal tokens = [t for t in tokens if not t.isdigit()] Example: ["flight", "123", "delayed"] ‚Üí ["flight", "delayed"] STEP 5: Stopword removal stopwords = {"the", "is", "a", "an", "and", "or", ...} tokens = [t for t in tokens if t not in stopwords] Example: ["the", "flight", "was", "great"] ‚Üí ["flight", "great"] STEP 6: Short word removal tokens = [t for t in tokens if len(t) >= 3] Example: ["a", "big", "jet"] ‚Üí ["big", "jet"] STEP 7: Lemmatization For each token t: t_lemma = lemmatize(t) # Convert to base form Example: ["flying", "flies", "flew"] ‚Üí ["fly", "fly", "fly"] STEP 8: Build vocabulary Count document frequency for each term Remove if: - Appears in < 5 documents (too rare, likely noise) - Appears in > 90% of documents (too common, no discriminative power) STEP 9: Final token list Return: cleaned_tokens = [t1, t2, t3, ...] Example transformation: Input: "The flight was GREAT! Flying with Emirates is amazing." üí° Why These Cleaning Steps Matter

Standardization: Without it, we can't merge data across sources or aggregate to airline level.

Deduplication: Prevents over-counting and bias toward frequently-duplicated reviews.

Missing data handling: Ensures we use maximum available information without introducing bias through improper imputation.

Text preprocessing: LDA requires clean token lists. Stopwords and punctuation add noise without meaning. Lemmatization groups related words (fly/flying/flew) so LDA sees them as the same concept.

2.4 Why Employee Reviews Have One Overall Rating

Understanding Glassdoor Data Structure

Question: Why do employee reviews have only one rating when customer reviews have multiple detailed ratings?

Answer: This reflects how each platform structures reviews:

Glassdoor (Employee Reviews):

  • One overall rating (1-5 stars): "How satisfied are you working here overall?"
  • Free-text pros: "What do you like about working here?" (qualitative)
  • Free-text cons: "What could be improved?" (qualitative)

So employees provide one numerical rating and two text fields.

Skytrax (Customer Reviews):

  • One overall rating (1-10 stars)
  • Multiple detailed ratings (1-5 stars each): seat comfort, cabin service, food, entertainment, ground service, WiFi, value for money
  • Free-text review: Detailed description of experience

So customers provide one overall rating + multiple detailed ratings + text.

For our analysis:

  • We used the overall rating (1-5) as the dependent variable in employee regression
  • We used LDA on pros/cons text to identify what drives that rating
  • We used the overall rating (1-10) as the dependent variable in customer regression
  • We used LDA on review text to identify complaint themes

Summary of Core Finding

We find evidence of a moderate positive relationship between employee workplace satisfaction and customer service satisfaction (r = 0.56, p = 0.095). While the small sample (n=10) limits statistical power, the effect size is substantial: airlines with above-median employee satisfaction achieve customer ratings approximately 0.4 points higher (t = 2.30, p = 0.071, Cohen's d = 0.97).

This finding aligns with service-profit chain theory and suggests that airlines investing in employee satisfaction may realize benefits in customer satisfaction. However, the correlational nature of this analysis prevents causal claims. Other factors (fleet quality, route networks, pricing strategies) also influence customer satisfaction.

5. Discussion and Implications

5.1 Theoretical Contribution

Our findings provide empirical support for the employee-customer satisfaction link in the airline context using large-scale review data rather than traditional surveys. The moderate correlation (r = 0.56) is consistent with meta-analytic estimates from other service industries (r ‚âà 0.40-0.60), suggesting the relationship generalizes to airlines despite their unique operational characteristics.

The topic modeling results reveal specific mechanisms: employee dissatisfaction with management, workload, and career opportunities predicts lower individual ratings, and these organizational issues likely spill over into customer-facing service quality. When employees cite "poor management" and "long hours with short rest," customers experience this as "delays," "boarding chaos," and "inconsistent service."

5.2 Practical Implications

For airline executives, these findings suggest that employee satisfaction initiatives should be viewed not merely as HR programs but as customer satisfaction investments. Specific actionable insights:

  • Management quality matters: "Poor management" emerged as the strongest predictor of low employee ratings. Investing in leadership development and management training may yield customer satisfaction dividends.
  • Workload and scheduling: Employee complaints about "long hours" and "insufficient rest" suggest that crew scheduling practices affect both employee wellbeing and potentially service quality. More reasonable schedules may improve both metrics.
  • Career development: Employees citing "limited growth" and "slow progression" show lower satisfaction. Creating visible career paths may improve retention and service quality.
  • Monitoring both metrics: Most airlines track customer satisfaction intensively but treat employee satisfaction as an internal HR metric. The correlation suggests both should appear on the same executive dashboard.

5.3 Limitations

Sample Size

Our correlation analysis relies on only 10 airlines, limiting statistical power and precluding strong causal claims. While the effect size is meaningful, replication with a larger sample would strengthen confidence.

Selection Bias

The 10 airlines with robust employee data are all large, established, full-service carriers. Low-cost carriers and regional airlines are underrepresented. Findings may not generalize to different business models.

Review Representativeness

People who write reviews are self-selected, potentially skewed toward extreme experiences. While large sample sizes (thousands of reviews per airline) mitigate this concern, the possibility of bias remains.

Temporal Misalignment

We aggregated reviews across time without strict temporal matching. If employee satisfaction changed substantially over the review period, this could introduce noise.

Causality

Correlation doesn't prove causation. While theory suggests employee satisfaction drives customer satisfaction, the reverse (successful airlines can afford better employee benefits) or third variables (good management improves both) are also plausible.

5.4 Future Research Directions

  • Larger sample: Expanding to more airlines, particularly in underrepresented segments (LCCs, regional carriers), would improve generalizability and statistical power.
  • Longitudinal analysis: Tracking how changes in employee satisfaction precede changes in customer satisfaction would provide stronger evidence for causal direction.
  • Department-specific matching: Linking cabin crew satisfaction specifically to in-flight service ratings, or ground staff satisfaction to check-in/boarding ratings, would sharpen the analysis.
  • Control variables: Including airline characteristics (fleet age, route network, business model) would help isolate the employee-customer relationship from confounding factors.
  • Cross-industry comparison: Testing whether this relationship holds in other service sectors (hotels, restaurants, healthcare) would reveal whether findings are airline-specific or broadly applicable.

6. Conclusion

This research asked whether airlines with higher employee workplace satisfaction also achieve higher customer service satisfaction. Using 42,599 reviews from Glassdoor and Skytrax covering 10 major airlines, we found evidence of a moderate positive relationship (r = 0.56). Airlines with above-median employee satisfaction achieved customer ratings approximately 0.4 points higher on a 10-point scale.

Topic modeling revealed that employee complaints about poor management, excessive workload, limited career opportunities, and inadequate compensation significantly predict lower employee ratings. Many customer complaints (delays, service inconsistency, ground service problems) appear to reflect the operational issues employees highlight, suggesting a plausible causal pathway from employee to customer satisfaction.

While sample size limitations (n=10 airlines) prevent strong causal claims, the pattern is consistent with service-profit chain theory and meta-analytic findings from other industries. Airlines like Singapore Airlines and Qatar Airways, which score highly on both employee and customer satisfaction, demonstrate that excellence on both dimensions is achievable.

For airline managers, the implication is clear: employee satisfaction is not merely an HR concern but a customer service imperative. Investments in management quality, reasonable work schedules, career development, and fair compensation may yield returns not just in retention but in customer loyalty.

We hope this research encourages airlines to monitor both metrics in tandem and motivates further research on the employee-customer satisfaction link using larger samples and longitudinal designs.
